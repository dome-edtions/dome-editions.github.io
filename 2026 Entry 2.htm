First Round of New Music

Week 2 of 2026: Music Recap

One week into the new year, it's time to check in on what's been happening (musically-speaking) in 2026.

Outside of music, it just seems like life is melting into chaos, yet again. However, I can always rely on the unpredictability of new times to bring on an equally varied and unexpected musical soundscape. Already this early on, I can tell just how strange 2026 is bound to be, though I'm unsure if I'll love it or hate it for it.

Regardless, this is an example showcasing how most of the blog posts will be organized throughout the year. I'll divide my topics into sections detailing the new albums / songs that released during the previous week, some artists and projects to look out for in the near future, as well as some first time listens of mine that need to be shared. Of course, I might start rambling about whatever feels apt at any given moment, but I'm getting ahead of myself.

For now, enjoy the first official musical recap of the year!

* * *

New Albums

Joost - Kleinkunst

What better way to start off the year than with some Dutch hardcore?

I can't say I ever heard Joost's previous work, but this seems like an accurate representation of his super zany character. With 24 new songs, Kleinkunst will definitely take you all over the place sonically. In a sense, it often sounds like a personal diary of sounds trying to emulate distinct worlds Joost traveled to during tours.

I can see counter-arguments against this album, but it's honestly not a bad listen to start off your year. It's certainly cheeky, it has some exciting beats, I love the Dutch-inspired humor he tosses in occasionally, and there are certified banger collaborations. One in particular that surprised me was Farmcore which features horsegiirL; it's such an oddball cut, but it also perfectly represents the cookiest moments on this album in one song.

* * *

CHUU - XO, My Cyberlove

I guess you can say I'm in my k-pop era these days.

Look, I'd like to formally apologize for anything negative I might've said about this genre before even giving it a chance. Lately, it seems like all I listen to on my down-time is k-pop related: I'm either jamming to Twice, alternating between Lisa and Jennie's biggest songs from 2025, or enjoying this new solo release from Chuu.

For those coming into this without much background (aka me), Chuu was part of the k-pop collective Loona, which explains a lot of the hype behind this new release. And honestly, it's a lot of tasteful bubblegum pop in the best way. Songs like Limoncella or Teeny Tiny Heart just fill that pop-demanding hole in my soul, and at 26-minutes in length, the entire album is a very trim listen that anyone could enjoy.

* * *

Ellende - Zerfall

Starting off the year strong for metal!

It won't take you very long to realize, but I'm a huge sucker for black metal above all other subgenres in the field. I still haven't exactly figured out why that is, yet I consistently gravitate toward higher-pitched vocals with fast-paced rhythms. To that extent, the Austrian band Ellende delivers on all my expectations for the new year.

Still, this isn't your conventional black metal album. At several points during the listen, it almost feels like the band dips into more classical arrangements with these beautiful strings layered on top of an underlying sonic chaos, which definitely made a lot of the earlier cuts stand out to me strongly. I'll definitely need to give this more listens in order to dissect its lyrics, but I'm for sure enjoying this from a sound-point as of right now.

* * *

Viagra Boys - viagr aboys (Japanese Deluxe Edition)

There you have it: the Viagra Boys are already active in 2026, releasing a deluxe edition of their self-titled album from 2025 featuring 4 new songs for fans to enjoy.

Personally, I found this album to be the most accessible from the band yet, and I'm really glad that I've been loving it. In fact, I like it even more now than I did when I wrote my year-end list for 2025 (I know it hasn't been that long, but it's still grown on me a lot). There's something about the careless attitude to the lyrics that makes these songs hilarious and punchy, which is why I was thrilled when I saw this deluxe edition pop up in my feed.

As far as the 4 new tracks go, they aim to land on the softer side. And again, I love how the band is getting very self-referential in their art, seemingly understanding their space in the world of music, making their truest intentions known through grotesque images and hyper-modern frames of mind.

* * *

Zach Bryan - With Heaven on Top

Seemingly, it's never early enough to set the bar for quality in country music for artists like Zach Bryan.

I'm always grateful when I'm reminded of this guy. Okay, I know that his personal record lately hasn't been amazing, but he addresses this himself all over this album. It's obvious that he's not willing to brush things off, confronting the heat head on, which I think is a bold move most artists wouldn't take.

Other vices Zach confronts on this album includes alcohol abuse, a topic that is heavily recurrent, coloring many of the songs here in a dour tone of recovery. I especially love the way he portrays this cycle of abuse through "jeans" that have circulated through his family tree, mentioning that he needs to chop down the tree to feel free, even going as far as to flee to Spain for a while in an attempt to correct his mindset by avoiding home.

The writing on the album also dives into current affairs, noting the worryingly divisive tensions in the USA while denouncing the way things are heading with respect to authoritarianism, gun violence, and whatnot. It's a stance that I don't hear many modern country artists adopt, which makes me like Zach even more.

To see both sides though, I think you'd be lying to yourself if you didn't think of the listen as bloated. As much as I love a good Zach Bryan album, 25 songs are a lot to commit to in one sitting, and there were definitely some moments that felt unneeded. Songs like Camper toward the back-end might offer some levity to the dark topics being discussed, but they surely don't make me feel like extending my stay just to hear him ramble about how sexy his new girl is. It definitely feels a little tone deaf next to all these thoughtful and introspective dissections of the self.

All in all, it's a bit early to say, but I think he's done it again.

* * *

Dry Cleaning - Secret Love

This is really weird... But I think I kind of like it?

I'll be honest, I've never heard music sound quite like this before. In one breath, it sounds almost like some of the more spoken-word cuts from Wet Leg's catalogue, then in another, it kind of rings like some of my current favorite abstract art-pop artists like Nadine Shah or even Cate Le Bon.

That last comparison isn't surprising given that Cate executively produced this entire album, to which I can attribute its drearily impressive sound. Between droning guitar loops, ambient passages that sound like post-music, and bare melodies to latch onto, it's not an easy listen to get involved with. Add to that these unhinged lyrics about hitting your head all day, eating burnt foods for pleasure, designing cruise ships to feel useful in society, dreaming about rocks, or anything else beyond absurdity...

There is indeed something strange about this album, yet I haven't been able to put it down lately.

* * *

SAULT - Chapter 1

I wasn't really there when the UK-based group SAULT initially rose to prominence in the early 2020s, but I will say that the collective's sound is feeling kind of dull this time around.

I suppose a lot of that revolves around the context in which this album was released. With Little Simz no longer associated with the project due to tensions with Inflo (which she delves magnificently into on her latest album Lotus), it seems like the producer is now left floundering with these peppy soul beats that feel anything but soulful. Not even Cleo Sol can save this album with her vocals given just how vapid a lot of the positive lyricism reads as on the record.

* * *

The Kid LAROI - Before I Forget

As much as I wish to love Laroi's music, there hasn't really been anything since Stay that has gripped me meaningfully in any way. I suppose it makes for an easy listen with a lot of softer passages, but that doesn't dispel the fact that its title is essentially a self-fulfilling prophecy: I already forgot about the album minutes past a first listen. Musically, there's not much of substance to back the actual themes and ideas he wishes to get across, which made it a lot more tedious than exciting to listen to.

* * *

New Songs

A$AP Rocky - Punk Rocky

Wait, this is actually happening!

I don't think there's any more backing out now for Mr. Rocky this time. Don't Be Dumb seems to be on the way (for realsies), and I'm definitely excited to see where we'll be taken. Granted, predicting what this album is going to sound like is becoming quite the challenge given just how freakishly different all of Rocky's latest songs have been.

This latest one Punk Rocky is more akin to indie / punk rock with a very soft vocal delivery, sounding unlike anything Rocky has ever put out before. While I'm a little bit thrown off that it's not sounding anything like Highjack, I can't say that I ultimately disliked what I heard; I'm just finding it so incredibly difficult to make any predictions about the upcoming album!

* * *

Cate Le Bon - Always The Same (with St. Vincent)

I'll take all the leftovers I can get from this latest Cate Le Bon era. Seemingly, she's been on an unmatched creative streak toward the end of 2025. Alongside releasing a full album to her name (which was a masterclass in art-pop), she also produced the Dry Cleaning album Secret Love that I mentioned earlier, and she's now moving forward with this new single that was written during the Michelangelo Dying rollout. Given that St. Vincent is on the track and I've loved the sound style from her latest album, it's no surprise that this song immediately gripped me among the new releases of the week. In its best moments, it almost feels like Cindy Lee was involved in part to produce such warm guitar riffs. And if this is the precursor to an eventual deluxe version sometime in 2026, sign me up!

* * *

Robyn - Sexistential / Talk To Me

Coming back in the 2020s with an album, Robyn has been steadily releasing singles in anticipation. However, I can't quite tell whether I should be excited or worried for this upcoming project after hearing this latest doublet of tracks. Talk To Me has this neat little electro melody to it, though it's nothing too novel, especially for this new decade. Then, Sexistential feels a bit out of touch, both in terms of how it sounds super plain and empty, but mostly because of the overly-raunchy ramblings about... Wanting to watch hentai...? Is that really what we're doing here? There's something really off about this second song which worries me given that it's the album's title track.

* * *

David Byrne & Olivia Rodrigo - drivers license

Look, I think this cover by David Byrne is mostly idol worship from Olivia's behalf, which is fine. The performance itself is a little awkward, but that doesn't detract from the warm sentiment driving the song. I'm much more excited by the prospect of a nearby remixed version of SOUR to be released in 2026 as a 5-year commemoration of the original. I can't wait to see who else Olivia recruits for the full tracklist!

* * *

Wesley Joseph - Peace of Mind (with Danny Brown)

This song right here was a legendary algorithm moment for me! I mean, it just appeared in my feed unannounced, and I'm glad it did. I never heard of Wesley Joseph before, but it only took one song for me to instantly get his sound, and I'm looking forward to his debut album coming out in April. Coming to you from the UK, he's bringing a much more atmospheric edge to hip-hop that is sounding super fresh for the scene he's originating from. Danny Brown's inclusion happens to be the cherry on top, though the song was already top-notch before his distorted "check" ad-lib could be heard echoing against the chill cloud of hazy production.

* * *

Sarah Kinsley - Lonely Touch

Well, it only took 1 week for the first of my predictions to turn out false. It seems like Sarah Kinsley is not actually gearing up to drop an album, but rather a 5-track EP by the name Fleeting.

Not that I'm upset about this... Well, maybe just a little bit. See, when your two lead singles are this high quality, I'll always be bummed to hear that we're getting less rather than more. Lonely Touch follows up on Sarah's previous single within this gloomier art-pop direction through which she is quite vividly yearning for love. It's a song that certainly lives up to the hype and emotion surrounding the teasers, and I'm all ears for the full EP when the time comes.

* * *

Bruno Mars - I Just Might

For the most part, this is pretty standard as far as Bruno Mars songs go. Though not as flashy as his Silk Sonic era was, it's still very poppy and full of character. Don't get me wrong, I'm not trying to act dismissive since an average Bruno cut is typically way more entertaining than most of the radio slop you can hear nowadays. For the full album though, I guess I would love to hear something truly different, whereas I Just Might feels like he's playing things a bit safe.

* * *

IDK - Life 4 a Life (with Pusha T)

While the track isn't necessarily the most lyrically ambitious in modern hip-hop, I can at least say it's a pretty smooth cut. Kaytranada's production definitely helps to elevate both IDK and Pusha's deliveries here, offering something of a more menacing vibe without disrupting the underlying sinister groove.

* * *

Ari Lennox - Twin Flame

While Ari Lennox remains one of the strongest voices for contemporary R&B, I can't say she's exactly displaying her truest potential on this new song. Sure, the atmosphere is pretty dreamy and sweet, but the lyrics feel excessively simple in their depiction of love, way simpler than they sounded on her 2022 album age/sex/location. Perhaps I haven't exactly been stunned by these latest singles, but I'm still looking forward to the full album coming out soon. Maybe I just need to hear these songs play out in sequence to fully capture her vision.

* * *

Tessa Violet - Knowing

Now that's a name I haven't heard in a while! The last time Tessa Violet crossed my mind was with her smash hit Crush all the way back in the pre-pandemic days. In the years since then, she's seemingly gone through a whole journey of self-discovery and whatnot, all of which she dives into on this new track. At its core, I really love the central message of "knowing this isn't enough is enough" that resonates throughout, and even musically it feels like something very new to hear. I'm definitely intrigued to see if more comes out of this era of Tessa's life.

* * *

Father John Misty - The Old Law

This new song by Father John Misty pretty much sticks to what makes him such a great artist. While not straying too far from the style of Mahashmashana back in late 2024, The Old Law sounds like a song that easily could've made that tracklist due to its similar structure, vocal performance, and abstract lyrical displays.
