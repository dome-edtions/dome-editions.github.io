Preamble

2025 has been a little strange compared to recent years. In a sense, I haven't gotten a real break since the start of summer.

Constantly being busy is a new-ish feeling to me, which is why I'm surprised I managed to maintain a regular schedule of new album listens. I guess that's something that won't ever change. So long as new music gets released, I'll always spare some time for discovery since it's something I enjoy doing too much to entirely disregard. I'd like to think it keeps me creative, or at the very least occupied as I wait for life to wrap back around.

And I suppose I haven't been that busy if I managed to compile these lists alongside fulfilling my other responsibilities. Listening to nearly 400 newly-released albums in one year is impressive. Although I don't mean to undersell that feat, what's even stranger to consider is that this number represents only a measly fraction of the uncountable selection of albums that released in 2025, and I've yet to fully appreciate just how tiny the dent I've made in the music world truly is.

This year, I've worked hard on expanding my genre collection, to varying degrees of success. I guess you can only venture so far beyond your comfort zone before your favorite releases start looking like all of your past obsessions. Still, I tried my best to redact lists that summarize each little sound pocket I've explored this year, from blends of shoegaze and black metal to progressive folk infused with a country spin.

As always, if I missed any obvious blind spots, do let me know, and I'll make sure to give them a listen in due time. Regardless, I hope you enjoy reading along because organizing, writing, and overthinking these lists is always a highlight of my year.

Who knows? Maybe you'll uncover something to drag into 2026 with you!

Deluxe Versions

To begin, I'd like to shout-out some deluxe album versions released this year that had me the most excited to revisit older albums I've always enjoyed. In no particular order, here are my top picks in this category.

* * *

Luna Li - When a Thought Grows Wings (Reimagined)

Though her original album back in 2024 didn't make my top 100 list at the time, it wasn't far off, and I still found myself sporadically revisiting tracks like "Confusion Song" or "Minnie Says" this year. I guess what really sold this reimagined version to me are all the tiny creative and musical updates Luna Li added to any given song, whether it be a slightly modified backing instrument or simply a stunning collaboration (most notably the reimagined version of "Fantasy" with Raveena). I was even a fan of that cute medley of jams near the end, and this updated tracklist makes me feel very strongly about her potential as an art pop great of the near future.

* * *

Lupe Fiasco - Samurai DX

To this day, Samurai remains one of my most played rap albums from 2024, so I was (obviously) thrilled to hear that it was getting an extension. Lupe took his sound in an even more instrumentally-smooth direction on this deluxe edition, providing us with jazzed-up remixes of "Samurai" and "Palaces" which are gorgeous. Not only that, but this deluxe package also featured great extra cuts that continue to prove that Lupe is still a top-tier lyricist and musical visionary.

* * *

Carly Rae Jepsen - Emotion (10th Anniversary Edition)

New CRJ is always something I'm up for! And with Emotion being one of the pop albums that defines me the best, hearing some extra tracks from this era made me feel nostalgic in a really warm and positive way. It was even nice to see "Cut To The Feeling" finally land on an official album, ending its reign as a single almost a decade later.

* * *

JPEGMAFIA & Danny Brown - Scaring the Hoes: Director's Cut

Back in 2023, the duo's original collab album went absolute insane, and it's still such an odd, funny, and chaotic listen whenever I decide to revisit it. That same year, they released a DLC Pack of 4 extra tracks which were neat, but they never really explained how the cuts were meant to intertwine with the rest of the tracklist.

That's where this Director's Cut thrives for me: I absolutely loved the way the DLC songs fit into the mix with the original material. To my ears, it makes the record's ending even more satisfying and well-paced. Even if the other 3 extra tracks tacked onto the end weren't anything too special, I still appreciate the resolved sequencing of the deluxe material!

* * *

JPEGMAFIA - I Lay Down My Life for You: Director's Cut

Speaking of JPEGMAFIA, he also released a very lengthy add-on to his 2024 rap-rock hybrid album super early in 2025. Despite many of the tracks ringing a little like noisy filler, I was surprised to see just how many great ideas were scrapped from the official album (especially that extended intro into the first song and the title track itself). Hearing fully uncut versions like this make me realize how difficult the trimming process before official releases must truly be.

* * *

SZA - SOS Deluxe: LANA

Technically this album's reissue came out in late 2024, but I'm counting it for 2025 since I listened to it a decent amount toward the start of the year. As much as SOS is an album that I'm still a bit mixed on, I'll at least admit that the deluxe tracks captured a style very reflective of SZA's music that I'm easily pulled toward. Plus, it was cool to hear her collaborate with Kendrick Lamar on "30 For 30" after seeing her massively contribute to GNX's success. Overall, I think this was a solid booster, despite feeling far removed from the original it's attached to.

EPs

Next up, I'll touch on several EPs that marked me throughout the year. Here, I considered any self-described "EPs" from artists that have piqued my interest enough to merit my curiosity for their future releases. I also included a fair share of familiar faces which are becoming regulars on my most-enjoyed lists. Generally, my appreciation of these selections increases as you near the bottom of this section.

* * *

Jae Skeese - 40 Hours

These days, it seems like Jae Skeese is always on the grind. Not only did he release relatively strong albums in 2023 and 2024, but he also dished out this collection of tracks in the first month of 2025, making for an impressive track record by today's standards.

On these 7 songs, he continues to garner support from me thanks to his clever imagery (especially on that "Rotary Phone" song) and solid beat selections. So, even if he's not necessarily reinventing his sound from ground level, I was happy to hear more from him on such short notice.

* * *

Logic - Aquarius III

As a quick follow-up to 2024's Ultra 85, these 5 new tracks expand on what made that album such a fun rap project. Maybe the first two tracks feature some weaker choruses, but I still hold that "Not A Game" with Lucy Rose's wonderful vocals and "Universe" with its captivating groove picked out the best sonic characteristics from this era.

* * *

TYLA - WWP

Essentially, these 4 tracks sound like some of the most energetic and upbeat cuts from Tyla's debut album back in 2024. Being as I was a big fan of that album, I carried over much of that same love toward this new EP of hers. What's interesting is that Tyla is sounding a lot more commanding and charismatic through her vocals, making her deliveries pop significantly (just listen to a song like "Mr. Media" and you'll hear what I mean).

* * *

BB Trickz - 80'z

I've seen this EP getting some strange backlash, so I definitely feel the need to come to its defense. I only heard of BB Trickz for the first time on the BRAT remix album in 2024, and listening to a full-ish project from her made me even more intrigued about her artistic style. She has this unconventional way of writing short songs with smooth flows and an almost-muted vocal track over which she raps / sings. Overall, I was pretty impressed with the uniqueness of her style, which makes me want to hear what she'll put out next (maybe in a longer form).

* * *

Yeat - Dangerous Summer

Dangerous Summer pretty much sees Yeat doing what Yeat does best. However, unlike on most of his albums where the rage-based production feels oversaturated and a bit aimless, the instrumental presentations on this EP work to elevate whichever feature artist lands on a given track, be it FKA twigs, Don Toliver, or any number of faces making an appearance on this tape.

* * *

Jane Remover - ♥

Even though most of these songs have been out for well over a year now, it's nice to hear Jane Remover finally compile them into an EP. When Revengeseekerz dropped earlier this year, I remember feeling sad that Flash In The Pan and Magic I Want U never landed on an official release, so I can rest assured that it happened at last. As for the EP itself, it is a lot more pop-friendly than you'd expect, featuring some really sweet melodies and lyrics that underscore Jane's most accessible and creative side yet!

* * *

Kilo Kish - Negotiations

Going in a synth / electro-pop direction with these new songs, Kilo Kish is sounding fantastic! The beats are crisp, her vocals are impressive, and there's a unity to the technological theming she struck on this particular EP. If anything, I'd love to hear her expand on this style in the future, even though it's already immensely enjoyable in its current state.

* * *

Mikayla Geier - HOT POT!

Immediately, I knew this EP was going to be amazing after hearing only the first song. Mikayla Geier is the type of artist you just know is gonna be big one day. Her over-the-top performances are only the tip of the iceberg, with her talent extending to creating genuinely sticky pop songs. There's not really a weak hook nor low point to be found here: HOT POT is just super impressive and to the point!

* * *

Kelly Lee Owens - KELLY

Techno done right! On her self-titled EP, Kelly Lee Owens really showcases what makes her an excellent producer with the sharp song structures of her club anthems. It really feels like she took the best instrumental jams and ideas from her 2024 album and compiled them into an EP that makes you want to move and jump along to every passing moment.

* * *

Ethel Cain - Perverts

Technically, this is an EP, even though it would rank amongst the longest pieces of music I've heard this year (if not the longest). Admittedly, I have not revisited this EP all that much, but listening to this for the first time marked one of my most memorable days back in January when it came out.

Here's what I'll say: listening to Perverts is not an easy task. Its soundscape is cold, uninviting, borderline terrifying, and you'll find yourself wondering whether your sanity is still intact the longer you listen. It features long spans without vocals with the space being filled up by droning noise and dark ambient pauses, but this precise combination becomes the perfect backdrop for gloomy morning wandering and contemplative nostalgia.

* * *

Nilüfer Yanya - Dancing Shoes

Unsurprisingly, my EP of the year.

I say unsurprisingly because of how highly I praised Nilüfer's 2024 album, placing it in my top 10 last year. In a way, this EP feels like remnants from the Method Actor era that sounded a bit too out-of-place to merge into the full album.

But as an EP, these 4 songs hit the spot. From the entrancing guitar rhythms on "Kneel" to the shoegaze (yes, you heard that right) influence all over the very fuzzy "Where To Look", Dancing Shoes feels like a long-lost companion to her 2024 album, one that adds a new dimension of versatile sound while retaining everything that made her lyrics so fascinating in the first place.

I can't even begin to describe how often I've played this EP; just know that it's more than a lot. Nilüfer Yanya is simply one of those generational artists that everyone needs to hear about.

Honorable Mentions

Of course, I like to round off this medley of introductory sections by naming a few of my honorable mentions for 2025. Although these albums didn't make my final top 100 albums list, I feel like they're still worth a mention for some reason or another. Without any further preamble (which there has been a lot already), here are the entries I picked out.

* * *

Offset - KIARI

An album that particularly surprised me in 2025 was this new one from Offset. While typically an artist that I've only enjoyed in passing or in short feature bursts, I was quite impressed with just how thoughtful the lyrics and beat selections were on this album. Perhaps not every guest nor tune is the most successful (especially given its rather lengthy runtime), but I still left the album with quite a handful of tracks that ranked among my favorite songs of the year, as well as with a renewed outlook on Offset as a commendable solo artist.

* * *

Mamalarky - Hex Key

Using high-contrast sounds and saturated melodies, Mamalarky made a bold statement with its particular pop brand that hits all the emotional highs and lows you'd expect from a modern indie album. If they were to refine their sound to be a bit more consistent across the runtime, I'm sure they'll soon be putting out some of the hardest hitting pop tunes of the next few years.

* * *

Jim Legxacy - Black British Music (2025)

Whether through melodic singing or with his signature UK hip-hop flow, Jim Legxacy is a force to reckon with on this new mixtape. I especially enjoyed how introspective a lot of his writing was on these tracks, tackling relationships, upbringings, and family dynamics, sometimes all in the same song!

* * *

Richard Dawson - End of the Middle

On End of the Middle, Richard Dawson continues doing what he does best using a noticeably moodier underlying sound. Often, it feels very dire yet inviting, almost as though he's gathering us all together before singing us a lullaby or putting a story from his past into light. I can't say that I was blown away by all the songs here, but the album made me curious enough to want to explore his discography in more depth in the coming year.

* * *

Jennie - Ruby

As much as I feel like Jennie is still growing and finding her own voice, Ruby is a great start toward achieving that goal. With some interesting feature combinations and a nice balance between energetic and moody, it feels like a compelling homage to herself. I bet she'll really wow us in the future when she works on perfecting her solo performances just a bit more.

* * *

Snõõper - Worldwide

This album's full of charm, and I really enjoyed how the band kept the tracklist succinct by trimming off whatever might have lacked in quality. Seriously, it's compact and full of energy from start to finish, which makes me want to hear even more from this uniquely rough-around-the-edges punk duo.

* * *

Panchiko - Ginkgo

To me, it sounds like the band is operating at its best on Ginkgo: the cute sonic palettes used on many tracks sound shimmering, the stickiness of the lyrics prevails across most songs, and there's a subtle comfort in hearing something so bright and positive for once.

* * *

Armand Hammer & The Alchemist - Mercy

Teaming up with The Alchemist yet again, Armand Hammer effectively doubles-up on their incredible album Haram from 2021. I will say, the group feels a little sleepier than usual, perhaps due to just how chill this particular set of Alchemist beats comes across as. There are nonetheless quite a few highlights to marvel at, notably the bright uptick on "Moonbow". I still would've loved to hear a tad more energy from everyone involved.

* * *

Sabrina Carpenter - Man's Best Friend

Contrary to what most might say, I actually preferred this to 2024's Short N Sweet. Maybe it's because I'm used to Sabrina's excessively-horny vibe by now, but her stick feels a lot more genuine now than it did last year. Another thing I've noticed is that the instrumentals themselves are a lot more varied, touching on disco and sounding country-inspired in other bouts. Though it's not exactly among my favorite pop showings of the year, I can't say that I was displeased hearing the hits being played on the radio this time around.

* * *

Laufey - A Matter of Time

Ok, you caught me! Rest assured though, this is not me performing. I'm just a sucker for Laufey's particular style of modern jazz, and this new album of hers continues this streak for me, admittedly with a little less novelty than when she first came on the scene. I also really loved the theming around time, clocks, and fairytales across the tracklist; it makes for a cute album to play on a calm evening.

* * *

Yeule - Evangelic Girl Is a Gun

A new Yeule album can't be a bad thing! Although this one doesn't feel as sonically cohesive as their works tend to be, there are still quite a few moments that kept me coming back occasionally (most notably the brilliantly poppy "Eko" and the one-two combo of the closing tracks that are just incredible).

* * *

Snocaps (Self-titled)

This new band largely directed by sisters Katie and Alison Crutchfield essentially recorded one of the more easy-going country albums of 2025. Though it's not as immediately captivating as Waxahatchee's Tigers Blood from 2024, its fun attitude and light-hearted approach to raw presentations keeps the listen refreshing every time.

* * *

KAYTRANADA - Ain't No Damn Way!

As much as this album lacks the punchier highlights from his more classic works, I still feel like Ain't No Damn Way acts like a transition back toward the typical KAYTRANADA-like aesthetics that I adored from his debut. Given just how purely this tracklist flows, its beats and grooves definitely make for a great tone-setter or background listen.

* * *

Blood Orange - Essex Honey

Similar to the previous album, I find Essex Honey to sound much better in the background given its generalized melancholic vibe. With generous contributions by Caroline Polachek, Mustafa, Lorde, and even Mabe Fratti, the soundscape feels rich, albeit a tad bit woozy and easy to get lost into.

* * *

Jenny Hval - Iris Silver Mist

With its whimsical aura and frosty atmosphere, Iris Silver Mist was one of my favorite art pop albums to return to in 2025, despite sounding a little more like backing noise at times. Using a similar soundscape you'd expect on any number of Julia Holter albums, Jenny Hval manages to weave these pillowy instrumentals using her voice to knit the whole experience together with some rambled afterthoughts.

* * *

Oneohtrix Point Never - Tranquilizer

Staying true to his signature sound, OPN's Tranquilizer is an hour-long journey into the depths of the bright producer's creative mind put on full display over this intricate set of instrumentals. Perhaps I've listened to this one a bit more passively than I wish I had, but it's still definitely worth a mention given just how often I played it toward this year's end.

* * *

Jane Remover - Revengeseekerz

Absolutely wild and entertaining, Revengeseekerz was impossible for me to avoid in 2025. Perhaps it's not the melodic pop album I was anticipating from Jane; it was nonetheless interesting to hear her venture into rage music with a distinctly experimental electronic approach. I guess the one downside to this album for me is just how saturated and overstimulating it sounds, which makes every listen feel a lot more overwhelming than I'd hoped. That doesn't discredit the massive highlights that really blew apart my mind and my year for the better!

* * *

Cameron Winter - Heavy Metal

Last, but certainly not least, a massive honorable mention goes to Cameron Winter's debut project Heavy Metal. Despite having enjoyed the last Geese album 3D Country, I wasn't too enthralled by this solo release back when it dropped in late 2024. It's only when I revisited the album later in the summer of 2025 that I realized I'd sidelined a truly remarkable display of lyricism, vulnerability, and uncategorizably strange emotion.

Obviously, this album is not for everyone; Cameron's odd singing alone might be enough of a roadblock to most listeners. However, if you are willing to give this project the time of day, I guarantee that you'll find yourself crying at 4 AM, repeating to yourself that you're nothing more than a 0$ man. Take it from personal experience, would you?

Main Song List

Before getting into my top albums, I also like to compile a list of my favorite songs in any given year. Obviously, I haven't listened to all the songs that released in 2025, that's just absurd (and I don't think anyone will ever be physically capable of achieving that).

However, I was relatively broad with my search, and I tried to capture a wide range of the music I've enjoyed throughout the year. Despite a lot of overlap with my albums list, I think there are a few hidden gems here that you might not have heard of. Again, I'd highly encourage you to check out any of the songs that intrigue you on this list!

* * *

100. John Glacier & Sampha - Ocean Steppin'

The gentle atmosphere on this groovy tune allows Glacier's soft rapping and Sampha's lovely vocal harmonies to meld impressively, in the process displaying two of the most exciting acts the UK has to offer within this realm of lowkey-coded hip-hop.

* * *

99. Lucy Dacus - Limerence

Though Forever Is a Feeling didn't land on my best albums list, it would definitely be a shame not to bring up Limerence, one of the key tracks on the record that showcases the specific sad-girl brand of music that describes Lucy Dacus the best. It's her sharp lyricism mixed with her ability to paint a compelling emotional portrait that sells the song so effectively.

* * *

98. The Marías - Back to Me

Building on what made Submarine such a successful lofi-pop album, the band delivered what sound like a few extra tracks from this album cycle earlier in 2025. Among these, Back to Me in particular stood out as having such a sticky vocal melody to it; I could listen to that hook all day long if need be.

* * *

97. Redveil - What3ver

Making a bid for the earliest hit of 2025 (having released just shy of the New Year), Redveil is sounding fully re-energized on this new single. Both in terms of his dynamic performance and the super creative instrumental backing the track, it's easily one of my favorites from him at this point!

* * *

96. Taylor Swift - Opalite

Honestly, after TTPD, I'm just glad to have enjoyed some crumbs of this new Taylor Swift era. Thanks to Max Martin's helm on the production side of things, many songs on her latest album actually sound vibrant, as any good pop tune should. My personal pick on the album would have to be Opalite due to its supremely catchy chorus and great underlying guitar tone.

* * *

95. Thundercat & Remi Wolf - Children of the Baked Potato

This song is as wild as the title lets on! With Thundercat's super fast-paced style of jazz meeting Remi Wolf's eccentric pop personality, the collaboration is definitely fireworks from the start, and it only makes me hope that they'll work together again someday.

* * *

94. Tyla - Mr. Media

Picking just one song as a favorite off Tyla's WWP EP is tough, and I still don't know if I made the right choice. Maybe that says more about the overall quality of the project, but I ultimately went with Mr. Media due to how charismatic she sounds in her delivery. I'd love to hear her uphold this level of confidence on a future album, that's for sure.

* * *

93. BB Trickz - Not a Pretty Girl

Using Clairo's Pretty Girl as an underlying sample, BB Trickz exudes character as she raps in this silky, almost hushed manner. The track is quiet, cute, really sticky, but unfortunately quite short. As I noted in the EP section, I really want to hear her expand on this type of sound in a longer format because there's a lot of potential here just begging to be optimized.

* * *

92. Wet Leg - Catch These Fists

It took me a while to come around to Wet Leg given just how peculiar their particular style of indie rock is, and I can confidently say that Catch These Fists is one of the songs that swayed me into seeing their perspective. It's such a banger with strong guitars and a constantly evolving vocal lead.

* * *

91. Armand Hammer - Super Nintendo

On their latest collaborative album, the closer Super Nintendo is a standout to me. The track essentially sees ELUCID and billy woods continue to excel in terms of rapping quality, but it's really the nostalgic beat akin to retro video games that makes this such an addicting listen.

* * *

90. Lil Nas X - Hotbox

Oh, we're getting funky with it! By far Lil Nas X's best song since Old Town Road, and I'm surprised he hasn't gone in a dance pop direction sooner given just how naturally his personality glides over this kind of beat. It's so smooth, and that Daft Punk line is among my favorite bars of 2025.

* * *

89. Squid - Building 650

With great strumming and guitar melodies to back such a powerful vocal front, Building 650 was one of the tracks that stood out among their album Cowards that released earlier this year, and I remember having played this one so much near the beginning of 2025.

* * *

88. Benee - Demons

The bossa nova edge on Demons truly is something else, and even Benee's cute vocals add to the mix in a way that left me feeling charmed. It's such a bubbly little track personifying love through demons, and it's definitely a standout from her latest album.

* * *

87. Carly Rae Jepsen - More

I'm keeping all the crumbs from the Emotion era! Like the title suggests, More is simply more of what we loved so much from 10 years ago, and hearing lost recordings resurface that mirror pop perfection from the last decade bring the biggest smile to my face. It's truly CRJ operating at her best!

* * *

86. Genevieve Artadi & Real Bad Man - Don't Gotta Think About U

As a lead single to their collab album Nothing to Worry About, this song really sets the tone for the type of cooky pop beats Real Bad Man produced to support Genevieve's zany vocal style. It almost feels like a rap song disguised as a pop tune in the strangest way, and I just love the ambiguity it emanates.

* * *

85. Rachel Chinouriri - 23:42

I'm in love with the bubbly beat on this track, and no one can dissuade me from that! Somehow, her Little House EP features some of the best songs Rachel's ever written, or at the very least some of my personal favorites. 23:42 is a fun and lively track all about being in love, and the timestamp kind of lets on the type of time when these thoughts were conjured, or at the very least repeated like a mantra before bed.

* * *

84. Chappell Roan - The Subway

Though it's not as fantastical as 2024's Good Luck Babe, Chappell's latest track The Subway is just about as grandiose in its declarations of love for someone who slipped away too quickly. It's an anthem about not having been able to say something soon enough, and regret looms long through the power in her voice.

* * *

83. Jim Legxacy - Father

This song might be straightforward and incredibly short, but Jim Legxacy's ambition goes a long way to make the track worthwhile. Father is all about relationships, whether with his own father or with some girl he wishes to share a true connection with, and all of this is rapped over such a crispy beat displaying Jim's brilliant ear for shiny melodies.

* * *

82. Jennie - Like Jennie

Holy banger of the year! What a breakthrough solo hit; I'm serious, this goes so incredibly hard. As hit or miss as some of Jennie's performances were on Ruby, the energy is pure on Like Jennie, to the point where I can't envision myself not dancing along, or at the very least bobbing my head along to the beat in admiration.

* * *

81. RAYE - Where Is My Husband!

RAYE returned in this year, and she's seemingly gearing up for a new album release sometime in 2026. With a lead single as entrtaining as Where Is My Husband, I can only imagine how strong her sophomore album will sound if she keeps sticking to this grand orchestra-backed jazz style. She truly found her calling in this lane, and I'm glad she's sticking to what brings out the dynamic performer within her.

* * *

80. Pink Siifu - Girls Fall Outta the Sky

As strange as Pink Siifu's latest album was, it surely led to some of the most experimental and ambitious pockets of contemporary hip-hop. Though there were several tracks fighting for this spot, I went with Gils Fall Outta the Sky due to just how effective this beat is at pulling you into its dramatic atmosphere. You really get a sense of dread whenever those drums finally kick in; it's so satisfying in that regard.

* * *

79. Sabrina Carpenter - Manchild

With Manchild, Sabrina Carpenter yet again managed to score a lot of mainstream plays on the radio and whatnot. However, I'll reiterate it again, I'm not too angry whenever I hear this get air in public because it's quite a fun song. I love how she leans heavily into country aesthetics which fit her voice incredibly well, and I also think it's one of the cheekier songs she's written that truly sells its humor.

* * *

78. Neggy Gemmy - Mysterious Girl

I don't think you understand how excited I was when I stumbled on this track accidentally then immediately fell in love with Neggy Gemmy's new sonic direction. After going heavy on the electronics on her last album, I loved hearing these woozier soundscapes full of ambiance with an underlying danceable groove that carries the whole track forward.

* * *

77. Laufey - Clean Air

A Matter of Time featured a lot of very classic sounding Laufey songs, though the one that stood out the most had to be Clean Air because its lyrics are just so witty and cute. Singing from the perspective of leaving a toxic relationship using visuals of "needing clean air" or "getting the fuck out of my atmosphere" is pure brilliance, and it's all performed so tastefully.

* * *

76. Haru Nemuri - Panopticon

Within Haru's extensive conceptual framework acting as the basis of her new album, I think what she proved above all else is how great she can sound over an electric guitar. Panopticon was one of the singles that had me the most excited to hear the full release given just how punchy the mix of instruments is, and even her chant-like vocals overtop make for an interesting recuring motif across the listen.

* * *

75. Lupe Fiasco - High Note

I'm telling you, Lupe Fiasco is just too talented for his own good. Samurai DX added a bunch of new tracks to expand the original album, though High Note has to be my favorite given its smooth progression and just how lush the vocals are sounding. Luv Moore's contributions to the hook also help to elevate the musicality of the ensemble, and Lupe continues rapping with his typical high level of finesse.

* * *

74. Gorillaz & Sparks - The Happy Dictator

The Gorillaz are back in their comfort zone on this single, and it leads to a very pleasant collaboration with legendary pop duo Sparks. The vocal chemistry between 2D and the guest performers is definitely what kept me coming back to listen to this song above all the other singles for their upcoming album, and I'm really glad to hear just how bubbly the instrumental sounds.

* * *

73. Miya Folick - Felicity

Wow, what a gorgeous song! Genuinely, that's the first thought that still pops up in my mind, no matter how many times I listen to it. There's just something so pretty about the drums, the vocals, the general sense of serenity transpiring through the subtle instruments ringing in the background. To my ears, this is handily Miya's most impressive song to date, and I'd love for her to expand on this formula in the near future. Gorgeous, indeed.

* * *

72. Silvana Estrada - Dime

There's a reason Silvana Estrada is one of a few select modern Spanish-speaking artists who managed to broaden their appeal to English-speaking audiences, and I think a lot of her success in that respect has to do with just how easy her music is to love. You don't really need to understand what is being said on Dime to love it, though that certainly helps to love it more. Through sheer vocal expressivity alone, Silvana paints emotion so vividly; it's quite a spectacle to hear!

* * *

71. Stereolab - Constant and Uniform Movement Unknown

Although I'd urge you to listen to Stereolab's full album to get a more complete picture as to what made the band so excellent this year, I definitely don't want you to miss out on this extra track that didn't make the final cut. Paired in a doublet with a similar song in nature, I really love how winding the melodic loop is, and the French-sung vocals are just so sticky, they'll actually tickle your brain for hours at a time if you're not careful enough.

* * *

Simbi was floating on this beat! Lading on a much more personal album, it makes sense that this track's topical focus is its main selling point. Essentially, the song uses verse 1 to explore the way love infiltrates into one's life, and verse 2 then complements this analysis by doing the same with fear. Taking these two verses together really works to contrast the way the two emotions tend to overlap in the closest ways, and I was really blown away by her lyrical prowess.

However, what I love even more is the way the song ends over these thumping afro-beat grooves with a sample that repeatedly shouts back the word "free" into the open. What ensues is Simbi using this sample to match the ending of all her lines with the words "free" or "three" in an interchangeable and incredibly playful manner. It's hands down one of the best closing sections to any song this year, and it fills me with a rush of energy each time!

* * *

14. Luna Li & Raveena - Fantasy (Reimagined)

Yes, Fantasy came out in 2024, but the reimagined version is fair game for this list! Besides, how can I in good faith ignore a track this beautiful? It just feels so wrong, so let me do my thing.

In all honesty, if Luna Li is not on your modern pop radar, you are doing something wrong. Having seen her take the initiative to reimagine her 2024 album into a more whimsical package was one of my favorite parts of the year. In particular, adding Raveena onto this song might just be the smartest musical choice of the year. I mean, the pairing just makes so much sense, why didn't we think of this sooner? Raveena brings a fairy-dust-like energy to the song, and the vocal harmonies she contributes are gorgeous. Not to mention, Luna Li herself tweaked certain production elements (most notably the bridge) to give the song a more light-hearted tone, and everything is executed to perfection.

Like I said before, I am anticipating great things for the future if this is the benchmark for what to expect next!

* * *

13. Clipse - The Birds Don't Sing

On a Clipse album no less, I didn't expect to encounter a song this beautiful.

Detailing the last conversations they respectively had with their mom and dad, Pusha T and Malice take you through these vivid descriptions of these final encounters. Pusha T discusses the regret of having missed a final Thanksgiving with his mother, Malice talks about his spiritual experience when he found his father passed away at home, and it's just a devastating song about finding a path forward without having anyone to guide you toward the light. The song even closes with a gut-wrenching final message from Stevie Wonder which will bring you to tears when you least expect it.

* * *

12. Jane Remover & Danny Brown - Psychoboost

Banger alert! Everyone stay calm... Or not, actually, permission granted to go crazy with it!

Maybe Revengeseekerz didn't make my top albums list this year, but that doesn't mean it didn't come packed with some of the most entertaining pop / rap / electronic music you'll hear in 2025. Picking just one song for this spot might've been a difficult task if Psychoboost didn't go absolutely insane every single time I play it. The beat Jane Remover created on this track is addicting, cycling through a whirlwind of electronic soundbites and lightning-fast effects that will have you spinning in an off-brand club. On top of all of this, Danny Brown comes in and effectively transforms the track into this rap crossover where he and Jane share verses in a mind-bending fashion. They have such great chemistry on this song, and I can just be glad that this collaboration came to exist.

* * *

11. Folk Bitch Trio - Cathode Ray

Lovely harmonies are at the core of what makes Folk Bitch Trio such a wonderful music act.

Cathode Ray is an exceptionally sweet song with an unwavering sadness woven through its guitars. The way it ramps up from simple acoustics to a full array of electric guitars and vocal harmonies is mesmerizing, no matter how many times I give this track a listen. Perhaps its message and lyrics delve a bit far into abstraction to be fully appreciated by most, but the girls nailed it for me on this one. As a fan of poetic writing and sad songs, it's a combination that just works for me, though it truly is the vocal layering and synchronized singing that stun me each time.

10. DJ Haram - Voyeur

Sneaking into the top 10 is this instrumental track from none of than underground producer legend, DJ Haram.

Now, you might be wondering how a song without vocals made it in the top 10, which leads me to believe that you've never heard this before. In fact, you do get 1 word to help understanding what you're hearing: voyeur.

If it were up to me to interpret the message behind this song, I'd say it crudely as follows: we are all voyeurs together, standing back watching the world getting fucked in front of our very eyes without intervening. Truth be told, I don't think that's too far off from the intended meaning.

Following from that logic, Voyeur takes on the form of a call for revolution, ringing like an anthem for large-scale systemic change. Given the utter chaos in this beat that incorporates blaring horns, thumping / glitchy drum patterns, and even a great deal of incomprehensible percussions, it does display the volume and angst preceding any wave of monumental change.

Honestly, I'm really impressed that I caught all those feelings off sound alone, but that's just the power of well-produced music with a strong driving message.

9. Wolf Alice - The Sofa

Speaking of songs with strong driving messages, how about you take a seat on this sofa next?

In 2025, Wolf Alice came swinging with their new album The Clearing, presenting a collection of songs each featuring an incredibly solid central premise or thematic focus. Trust me, picking just one song off this album was difficult, and I teetered around with all my options until I decided to opt for The Sofa shortly after beginning the list-writing process.

So, what exactly makes this song so special? At its core, it's a song about a girl singing about wanting to lay on the sofa and do nothing... But that's such a shallow overview, way oversimplifying the underlying message that sums up the album as a whole.

See, The Clearing is all about presenting you with various snapshots of time, whether pleasant, sad, nostalgic, or just plain passive. It doesn't really matter what the band sings about, but rather what moment in time they are discussing. On The Sofa, the lyrics seem to suggest that any emotion in the present is valid, and that one's experiences can only ever summate to the current moment in time. It doesn't matter if you're having fun, crying, falling in love, or if you just want to kick back and lay on the sofa for a little while.

In essence, it's a song about being okay with the present and coming to terms with a linear passage of time that will soon swipe it away.

* * *

8. Geese - Au Pays Du Cocaine

Hey, can you play me that one song that keeps making me cry... Yes, the one about the sailor on a big green boat!

Okay but seriously, why is this song so depressing to listen to? Among a tracklist filled with intense grooves, funky guitar passages, fiery vocal performances, incredibly persistent drumming, is it really the slow jam near the midpoint that I put on this list? Yes, and deal with it.

Au Pays Du Cocaine is a wonderful song because it doesn't need to make sense for it to resonate with you. Does it matter that Cameron Winter is singing about craving the life of a sailor on a big green boat? Does it matter that the second verse is just the lyric "you can change" repeated about a dozen times? Does it matter that the song title may or may not have anything to do with the song's content?

To those questions, I answer a big, fat, enthusiastic no. None of these factors matter because, at the end of the day, the song makes me feel like a very sad boy whenever I put it on, and sometimes the emotional appeal of a tune is worth a whole lot more than whatever message it's trying to get across. See, the vocal performance on this song isn't necessarily pretty: it's gravelly, off-pitch, croaky, odd... But it feels human. I don't know about you, but I'd rather listen to the Cameron Winter whine and moan his way through this song with the utmost misery instead of modern rock songs with processed vocals and artificially-flavored instrumentals.

This right here, this is real. It's not pretty, but the band keeps it real, and I appreciate that.

* * *

7. Horsegirl - In Twos

Speaking of songs that make me want to cry...

Yes, I'm using that segue again, and I'll use it a third time if I'm in that mood. Besides, I feel like the reasons I love the Geese track right before this also apply to this Horsegirl song. My appreciation for these tracks stems from the same corner of my heart, and the fact that I'm often feeling like a sad boy definitely helps to bridge the gap between these two very distinct musical worlds.

Again, In Twos is not an exceptionally exciting song off the band's latest album. In fact, I'd call this the most linear and monochrome of the bunch, driven by a very simple guitar lick that loops indefinitely with some dreary vocals layered on top of its simple melody. And maybe it's this idea of simplicity that I'm trying to emphasize through these last two picks: songs don't need to reinvent music itself to sound good... They just need to sound good.

In Twos is a sad song because it carries what feels like years of regret after living in a small town. It also sounds like the result of feeling hopeless for the future, or lost in the present, or sad about a big change in your life that is about to occur. It sounds like graduating from school when you just want to stay put, saying goodbye to your friends when you won't see them for a while, walking along the pier late at night when it's just a bit too cold.

See, this is a song that makes you feel human. It connects you to yourself by allowing your mind to wander over its simple tune. This is a song to play whenever you need that little moment of clarity to make it to tomorrow.

Simply put, it's a song that makes me feel alive in a depressing kind of way.

6. Model/Actriz - Acid Rain

Wow, I'm really going for 3 sad songs in a row. I guess I was in that kind of mood this year.

I mention this later on my album list, but I found myself enjoying the deep cuts on Pirouette quite a lot more than I did those on Dogsbody. If anything, I feel like this is an area the band improved significantly in between albums, and I'm rewarding that improvement with this placement.

Acid Rain reads like a tragic fallout between two lovers, with lyrics having the song's protagonist still reminiscing about this relationship years later. It comes with a plethora of lyrics that hint toward this person having been the inspiration behind turning to music as a getaway, but the band puts it into way more poetic terms than I could ever conjure on my own. "I sing in part because you often told me that you liked to listen" might just be the most heart-wrenching lyric I've heard this year. It tears me apart every time the chorus circles back around, especially on that last iteration when it's performed acapella in a falsetto.

Instrumentally, the song also sees the band performing at their best, maintaining their typical tension in the drums and guitars, only this time conducted with a much more dire tone to feed into the drama. I even love the eerier section near the end which ramps up in typical Model/Actriz fashion before breaking out into the bare finale I mentioned above.

Overall, just a phenomenal love song, definitely one of the most bittersweet you'll cross in your lifetime.

5. Deftones - Infinite Source

Heading into the top 5, I'll kick things off with the best song on the new Deftones album: Infinite Source.

The concept of the track is relatively straightforward, pretty much reading like a love letter to being on tour, to the fans, to music itself more broadly too. It's a great idea, but the execution is truly what sets this song apart from many others attempting a similar angle.

Vocally, Chino still sounds incredible, even on moodier songs like this one where he's forced to alternate between melodic and borderline-metal vocal styles. He does a supreme job at matching the energy of the underlying instrumental, gradually ramping up in intensity as he travels from the verses to the chorus. Even the bridge is masterful at creating space for emotion to brew and for his vocals to seep back into a gloomier tone. Instrumentally, this track is equally as exceptional for all the same reasons.

I guess it's a bit difficult to explain what makes this song so great because it's just so obvious in my mind. Anyone should be able to enjoy it thanks to its warm guitar tone, lyrics that morph into any meaning you attribute to them, and impressive vocal deliveries. It's quite simply the whole package, and I don't think there's too much more for me to add on. As such, I'll let the music speak for itself instead!

4. Quadeca - Godstained

At number 4, I'm going to give it to Quadeca with the lead single to his latest album: Godstained.

If 2025 showed me anything, it's that lead singles carry a lot of weight when igniting the album rollout for any artist. Many of the songs on this list are present because of how well they laid the groundwork for their respective rollouts, such as Samia's Bovine Excision I mentioned earlier. I can say this is the case of Godstained too which predicted the path that Vanisher would take on...

That path being unpredictability reincarnated in a musical format. To me, what makes Quadeca such a unique artist is that he's great at blending genres together until they sound indistinguishable from one another. That's what made Scrapyard such a cool mixtape, and it's also what makes Godstained such an impressive song, sounding like a condensed version of the experimentation from his previous works packed into a single song.

Starting off as a melancholic pop tune with a pleasant vocal melody, the song quickly transforms into a medley of soft rock, quick-paced rapping, flute-backed baroque, and all of this is performed with background sounds emulating the sea. Seriously, the theming of the track (and more broadly of the album) around water is so creative, and I'm glad Quadeca really stuck with it, delivering on the concept in a big way.

Overall, I think this is very deserving of a spot in the top 10, and I'm still not sick of playing this one almost a year past its initial release.

3. Hannah Frances - Falling from and Further

With a lead single sounding this warm, I knew I was in for an incredible album.

Listening to Falling from and Further sounds like a hot sunny day molded into sonic form. That's the best and most accurate way I can describe this song's sound, and it also extends to a great deal of the material on this album.

Rewinding a bit to add some context, Hannah Frances is an artist I was introduced to for the first time in 2024 who writes some of the best contemporary folk songs the modern world has the chance to hear. Last year, she released Keeper of the Shepherd, a masterfully-written album concerning themes of loss and regret, all of which was underscored by Hannah's incredibly raw and powerful vocals.

That's probably why this new single surprised me so much. Contrary to the cold and hostile nature of the last album's sound, this song is instead wrapped in something so cozy. It shares a very similar drumbeat pattern to many of the last album's highlights, but it has a distinctly inviting and positive quality to its horns and upbeat progression. Even thematically, it writes about being on the other side of grief, having overcome loss and battling forth for a better tomorrow instead of constantly falling further into emotional turmoil over circumstances that can't be reverted. And after all the sad songs listed right below this one, I guess it's a nice change of pace to showcase some optimism.

I could say more, but I'll be speaking about the album as a whole in greater detail in the next section. Just know that I was incredibly close to giving Hannah the title of song of the year for a second year in a row!

2. Maruja - Saoirse

What a phenomenal build!

I might not have been clear enough, so let me repeat: what a phenomenal build on this song!

If you've been tapped in with upcoming rock bands, then Maruja is probably a name you're familiar with. And if this is the first time hearing about them, buckle up, and get really to hear some life-changing songs.

After a string of EPs featuring some intense post-rock tracks with spoken word vocals detailing stories overtop, the band's style became cemented with an appeal hovering around the expansive and incremental nature of their songs. Each song up to now required an impressive build, gradually climbing up to an explosive finale, and their debut album Power to Pain follows this formula closely.

However, it only took me 2 listens to weed out one track among the others that felt special. Saoirse initially charmed me thanks to the beautiful sung melody of its lyric "it's our differences that make us beautiful" that gets repeated over and over throughout the listen. On impact, I thought this was such a wonderful message that more people need to hold as true, and it plays into the album's themes of bridging divides between ostracised and radicalized societal bubbles. Moving forward, recalling what makes humanity or societies beautiful requires a recontextualization of this lyric in a way that benefits all of us.

After a few more listens where my aim was primarily to get familiar with the lyrics, I grew really attached with the way the song builds tension for about 2 minutes straight before a single word gets uttered. It makes for a period of silent reflection during which listeners can ponder over the intensity of the previous song, breathing in before the next societally-mandated adrenaline spike.

It's also worth pointing out just how impressive the vocal tone is on this track. Every single lyric within the verses is uttered with so much conviction, I wish more modern rock bands would be as emotionally invested as Maruja is on this song. I suppose it's this level of passion that drives the climactic finale with those horns and soaring leads that vanish into a calm aftermath.

Phenomenal doesn't really do the song justice. It's a masterpiece, and easily Maruja's best song yet!

1. Japanese Breakfast - Honey Water

After a lot of consideration about what song should land at this spot on my list, I finally settled on giving Japanese Breakfast the top spot, and I have several reasons backing this decision.

For starters, picking a Jbrekkie song to add to this list was a tough enough task to begin with. I had Mega Circuit locked into the top 10 for the better part of 2025 due to its fun groove, sticky vocal melody, and great commentary on rejecting and ridiculing toxic masculinity archetypes.

Near summertime, I kind of forgot that this album existed as I gradually made new discoveries, but something pushed it back my way come the beginning of a new semester. With slightly cooler temperatures and gloomier days ahead, a great deal of the songs on this album began speaking to me. Some like Picture Window or Winter in LA depicting a person stuck inside, not daring to venture in the outside world to discover what life lay beyond, they really struck me in a new light, especially as I finally began turning my life around for the better.

Men in Bars is yet another song that I've only recently become obsessed with, Orlando in Love features a beautiful opening message to set the tone for the album, and (really) just about any song on this album was seeming like a contender for this spot. That's probably why I ended up putting Jbrekkie on top: anyone writing an album with this many song of the year contenders should downright deserve the spot.

However, what really sealed the deal was when Honey Water clicked for me. I'm not sure why it took so long to grow on me given that I've always loved the rockier cuts on older Japanese Breakfast albums, but I inevitably fell in love with the sound Michelle and the band present on this song. The appeal for me is immediate because the guitar rhythm that persists throughout the track kicks off instantaneously. Over this instrumental, she sings about an unfaithful man, just another toxic relationship that seems to be clouding her better judgment...

Quite literally too. The band goes the extra mile in the second half of the track to portray this mind fog brought forth by the relationship, exploding into a fully-fledged shoegaze song with distorted electric guitars, messy drums, strings that sound like they're coming to you from all directions, blaring saxophone runs that get progressively louder... It is such a clusterfuck of noise and chaos, actively depicting a mind filled with too many thoughts, yet lacking the cohesion to do anything about all the mental burden. Just hearing the escalation in this track go on for way longer than you'd anticipate gives me such a thrill, I can't get over just how blissful the song's climax is!

To compliment the song further, Honey Water sounds like my bloody valentine reincarnated in the year 2025, and that's pretty high praise coming from someone who considers them to be one of the best bands to ever do it. Also, I want to point out that Japanese Breakfast have been putting out exceptional songs like this one for the entire decade. Not too long ago in 2021, I might have placed Be Sweet at that number 1 spot had I been making lists back then. I guess that just goes to show that, whether it's synthpop or shoegaze, some bands just possess a raw talent that can't be faked.

So, yes, I'm confident in this placement... Or maybe my mind's just been a bit foggy lately, causing me to perceive this song's noise as my own!

Main Album List

Here we go, the moment I've been building up to for all of 2025. Truth be told, this is always the most exciting part of my year, and it's fascinating for me to look back at these lists several years down the line to see whether I was right at the time, or whether I'd missed some fairly obvious picks (I usually skip a couple that turn into dire regrets; re Cameron Winter).

I guess there's only one way to find out how my predictions hold up. It's countdown time!

* * *

100. PinkPantheress - Fancy That

Once again, PinkPantheress returns to deliver a succinct set of fun bops, and after hearing her 2023 album, I can't say I expected anything less! At just 20 minutes in length, it proves to be a manageable listen at pretty much anytime, and I'm glad to hear her still reigning uncontested in the lane of creating cutesy short songs.

* * *

99. Tennis - Face Down in the Garden

With this presumably being their final album as a duo, Face Down in the Garden sounds like a pleasant retrospective on Tennis' music career, highlighting their knack at writing compelling love songs with vivid stories and a vintage pop production that leaves everything sounding almost like a distant memory.

* * *

98. Skrillex - F*ck You Skrillex You Think Ur Andy Warhol But Ur Not

On his latest release, Skrillex takes things back in a traditional EDM direction. Not only are the various drops he curated exciting, but the project's flow also makes this feel more like a live DJ set that you're experiencing in the moment, and the humorous one-liners peppered in by DJ Smokey makes this one of the funnier listens of the year.

* * *

97. The Beaches - No Hard Feelings

Maybe this album doesn't reflect the highs of the Canadian girl-band's breakout album Blame My Ex in 2023, but it still showcases their ability to put together a damn good batch of tracks that stick to a consistent theme of queer-drenched heartbreak within sour love ventures.

* * *

96. Marina Satti - Pop Too

Using her Greek heritage to breathe new life into modern pop music, Marina Satti is quickly becoming a pioneer for the genre with every new release. The production she presents is incredibly novel, and most of her appeal stems from just how immediately poppy many of her vocal lines are.

* * *

95. Tyler, The Creator - Don't Tap the Glass

Despite not being as introspective as 2024's Chromakopia, introspection is not the intention driving this newest drop from Tyler. In fact, what you should expect from Don't Tap the Glass are fiery house beats, unfiltered rapping performances, and one of Tyler's most succinct yet well-defined eras so far!

* * *

94. Mariah The Scientist - Hearts Sold Separately

In today's climate, Mariah The Scientist continues to be a pioneer for R&B sounds that tug at the genre's root, and she possesses both the vocal talent and smooth execution needed to back her contemporary spin on things.

* * *

93. Rich Brian - Where Is My Head?

From YouTube sensation to Edamame with bbno$, it was nice to finally hear this more personable side of Rich Brian. You can really tell just how hard he's worked to get to a point where his production sounds as crisp as it does on Where Is My Head, and his reflections on his upbringing, social relationships, and success are plentiful.

* * *

92. Black Country, New Road - Forever Howlong

After having reinvented themselves in 2023 with Live At Bush Hall, one of the UK's most exciting new-age rock bands is back in full form on Forever Howlong. What's even more interesting to me is just how theatrical the instrumental builds and vocal performances sound this time around, making the album read more like a homespun play in its best moments.

* * *

91. Kali Malone & Drew McDowall - Magnetism

A collaboration between organist Kali Malone and experimental producer Drew McDowall isn't something I expected to hear in 2025, but the resulting 5 tracks lead to one of the most compelling ambient noise albums I've heard in quite a bit. I'm fascinated by just how desolate and isolating these drones feel when listened to in utter solitude and silence.

* * *

90. Lily Allen - West End Girl

I don't think I've ever heard a breakup album quite like this one before. Marital affairs, open relationships, Madeline-related shenanigans... There's a lot Lily Allen unpacks here, and it's done so well that I keep revisiting the album in hopes of getting the storyline right, all while enjoying the upbeat pop production soundtracking the drama.

* * *

89. Ela Minus - DÍA

What captivates me most about Ela's most recent work is the way she integrates electronics into the flow of her music. It's interesting to hear her take a super holistic deep-dive into her psyche and emotions while underscoring what feels like a set at the club.

* * *

88. Anna von Hausswolff - Iconoclasts

This massively daunting post-rock album nearing 80-minutes in length sounds like the result of many years of honing the balance between ambient pauses and epic saxophone explosions. The deeply cold and unsettling drum patterns become this album's heartbeat in the unlikeliest moments, and it truly emulates an admirable ambition.

* * *

87. The Last Dinner Party - From the Pyre

While The Last Dinner Party doesn't present a tracklist flooded with snappy bangers like they did on Prelude to Ecstasy in 2024, the glam rock band still carries over a lot of the initial appeal from their debut onto their sophomore album. Maybe they stoke the flames of drama a little too much this time around, but that doesn't discredit just how impressive the themes, instrumentals, and visuals are all over the record.

* * *

86. Mei Semones - Animaru

Juggling pristine vocal passages in both English and Japanese, Mei Semones sounds charmingly shy while displaying tremendously technical guitarwork all over her foray into jazz-pop soundscapes. It's up there with the cutest albums you'll uncover in 2025!

* * *

85. El Michels Affair - 24 Hr Sports

Brought to you from the same producer behind the genius that was Clairo's Charm back in 2024, 24 Hr Sports illustrates that El Michels Affair's versatile ear for beats always leads to pretty instrumentals that can make any feature artist shine. Not only that, but even the solo instrumental cuts help to create a lush listening experience that highlight Leon Michel's distinct musicality.

* * *

84. McKinley Dixon - Magic, Alive!

On Magic Alive, McKinley Dixon is upping his conceptuality by imagining himself as capable of bringing spirits back to life. With a ton of smart feature curations and forward-thinking jazz-rap instrumentals, you won't want to miss the narrative woven through its lyrics.

* * *

83. Sumac & Moor Mother - The Film

Listening to The Film acts like a stark reminder that Moor Mother is aiming to have the most diverse discography of the 2020's decade. This time, she joins forces with the sludge metal band Sumac, allowing for her poetry to take on a remarkably eerie tone over these grimy and winding rhythms. Not one to miss if you're a fan of droning guitars, expansive songs, and cinematic arrangements.

* * *

82. Melody's Echo Chamber - Unclouded

Despite operating within a predictable range of sounds, Melody Prochet makes every moment count on Unclouded with fantastic drumming arrangements, soft pillowy vocals, and a smooth indie pop tone. Given how late this released in 2025, it'd be a shame to let something so fuzzy and bright slip you by!

* * *

81. Alessia Cara - Love & Hyperbole

Are you really a Canadian if you're not listening to Alessia Cara? On her latest album, she's sounding as sharp as ever over a collection of slick drumbeats reminiscent of old soul records with an unwavering topical focus on messy love.

* * *

80. Imperial Triumphant - Goldstar

With it being so trim in length, this is not a metal album you'll want to miss in 2025. At this point, Imperial Triumphant pretty much owns its distinct lane of metal, far removed from all other acts thanks to its use of horns and jazz-like elements. Even when they are sticking to the core of death metal aesthetics, the band sounds incredibly polished and energized from one track to the next.

* * *

79. Mac Miller - Balloonerism

On Mac Miller's 2nd posthumous album, his legacy is expanded as we remember what made him such a special artist. It's interesting to hear that, even early on in his career when these songs were first written, his abstract ear for uniquely thoughtful (and sometimes eerie) production as well as his masterful pen truly let you envision his demons as though your own.

* * *

78. Franz Ferdinand - The Human Fear

Keeping in tune with what made the band so entertaining during their come up, Franz Ferdinand's cheeky song ideas and positively upbeat instrumentals maintain The Human Fear as one of the most fun albums to keep revisiting, even almost a year after its release in January. I guess you can say the band had me Hooked from the get-go! (okay, for real, I'm done with the cheap puns... maybe...)

* * *

77. Viagra Boys - Viagr Aboys

If this counts as a self-titled album, then I wholeheartedly agree that Viagr Aboys captures what the band is best at: making you question what the fuck you're even listening to. Amidst borderline incoherent ramblings about flappy giblets, croutons underneath futons, and eating insects to slow down your immune system (slowly), the band is sounding better than ever against their signature punky guitars and insanity-riddled songs.

* * *

76. Jade - That's Showbiz Baby!

Among the many breakthrough artists of 2025, Jade proved to be one of the most prominent in her approach to creating worthwhile dance music. Being inspired primarily by the greats of disco (most evidently Diana Ross with that gorgeous sample nod near the album's end), it's no surprise that many of the sounds and song ideas pan out marvellously in her favor on her debut.

* * *

75. Rosalía - LUX

You could argue that Rosalía delivered the most ambitious album of the year given just how impressive the orchestral arrangements are all over LUX. Even from a linguistic perspective, hearing her sing beautifully in Spanish, Italian, Japanese, Latin, and so many more languages makes it seem like she's vocally flexing on us. All the while, she somehow makes these combinations work as though orchestrating the score for the next grand ballet in the process.

* * *

74. Haru Nemuri - ekkolaptómenos

With bold political convictions and an untamed exploratory spirit, Haru Nemuri really went all out when curating this newest album. Admittedly, I wasn't enthralled by the totality of the experimental choices she stuck to on some of the songs, but that doesn't shy away from the fact that the consistency in terms of lyrical ferocity and sonic experimentation lead to some of her highest highs yet.

* * *

73. Chat Pile & Hayden Pedigo - In the Earth Again

As far as odd pairings go, the union of sludge metal band Chat Pile and acoustic guitarist Hayden Pedigo is yet another match that seems too strange to even click. Then again, it only takes one listen of In the Earth Again to realize just how well Hayden's delicate strings work to amplify Chat Pile's monstrous sound. Effectively, the duo learns to balance the calm and the storm in a manner that's epitomised by this album's brilliant tonal shifts.

* * *

72. Tyler Childers - Snipe Hunter

Modern country that doesn't sound like the tasteless pap in the mainstream! Truthfully, Tyler Childers has a great deal to say and a whole lot of heart driving the themes of his songs. From his appreciation of Hinduism's philosophical practices to tales of remaining hardworking in the face of darkness, he puts a lot of thought into his messages without overlooking the power of varied vocals and rocking instrumentals in the process.

* * *

71. Conway The Machine - You Can't Kill God with Bullets

Disclaimer: this probably would've landed higher if it didn't release just a few weeks before the end of the year. After a few years of inconsistent output, Conway The Machine is sounding especially introspective and hungry again on You Can't Kill God with Bullets. Beyond such a fantastic exposition to the whole project, Ghais' rough and homespun production keeps the album feeling accessible, and his pen game leaves every bar and chorus running laps in your mind when the music stops.

14. Kali Uchis - Sincerely,

It's just so beautiful!

Kali Uchis takes you from one gorgeous tune to the next on Sincerely, which might just be her best album yet. Maybe it doesn't feature a star-studded cast like on some of her previous releases, but it's definitely a story for only one person to tell. Conceptually, it is inarguably her most mature batch of tracks dealing in love and family in the most concrete light so far.

Thematically too, I love how the album is shaped almost like a love letter to her artistry and to her family, and all the song titles uphold this format within a compelling structure. Also, the immaculate sequencing makes me wonder whether any other albums from 2025 sounds as good as this one during a continuous listen.

13. Backxwash - Only Dust Remains

12. Marina Sena - Coisas Naturais

It too is just so beautiful!

Marina Sena is an artist I absolutely fell in love with this year, not just because she's charismatic and stunning, but also because her music has such a charming allure to it. Coming to you straight from Brazil, Coisas Naturais is an album that blends traditional Latin pop, a bit of reggaeton, synthpop, and a whole lot of contemporary pop featuring a great deal of unique experimental sounds.

On top of just how pretty the whole package is wrapped in, its writing is simply incredible. The way Marina interleaves nature-based imagery into her experiences treading with love is just so impressive. She basically weaves through emotional complexity unlike any other artist has this year, and it's organized so tastefully, I can't help but fall in love just a teeny tiny bit.

11. JID - God Does Like Ugly

I'm telling you, JID simply cannot miss! He's one of my GOATs for a reason!

After releasing what I still hold to be the decade's strongest hip-hop album in 2022 with The Forever Story, my hopes for JID matching that level of quality were very dismal. And yet, despite my initially low expectations, I feel bad that I ever doubted him.

Maybe God Does Like Ugly isn't the flawless masterpiece that JID's last release was; it's nonetheless a conceptual reiteration of what makes him such a fantastic rapper. On the search of what makes one ugly, he takes us on a journey into his mind, through the depths of Hell and back in the process. Not to mention, he never falters even slightly on the performance side, offering one electric flow after the next, surgically weaving through slick bars with ease.

And on top of all this, he's expanding his musical side as well, operating over novel kinds of beats that have no business working so well in tandem with his quick-paced deliveries. It truly is an inspirational album, landing just shy of my top 10 this year!

10. Quadeca - Vanisher, Horizon Scraper

Kicking off the top 10, Mr. Quadeca himself came through with what I consider to be his masterpiece.

I was already a pretty big fan of 2024's Scrapyard, but Vanisher takes Quadeca's artistry to a whole new level. From the cinematic backdrop provided by the full-length film accompanying the album, it really sounds like undertaking an entire journey when listened to in full.

One feature I absolutely adore about this album is the consistent theming of the tracks with a bunch of ocean- / water-based elements. From waves rustling through the tracks to lyrics about a nautical voyage, it sounds like the most compelling exploration of a concept that I've heard in 2025. I especially love how this motif is maintained up until the end when you can pretty much hear the protagonist getting invaded by the waters, essentially drowning after undergoing such a long and perilous quest.

Theming aside, the musicality underscoring the project is just as impressive to me. Something Quadeca did incredibly well on Scrapyard was exploring various genres within a loose framework. After having tested these styles last year, it really sounds like he took the best of these explorations and composed genre-bending combinations on tracks like "Godstained" which are almost impossible to define stylistically. Even on the vocal front, Quadeca delivers one stunning performance after the next, absolutely nailing the slower tracks like "Monday" or even the gigantic "Forgone".

It's just a special album, and I can definitely get behind the Quadeca hype at this point.

9. Ninajirachi - I Love My Computer

I sure do love girl EDM!

There are few albums in 2025 that managed to strike a balance between being fun, accessible, and conceptual in the way that Ninajirachi achieved on her debut. I mean, let that sink in: this is her first album, and it's already beyond fantastic. Imagine how great things have the potential to be!

I Love My Computer is obviously an ode to Nina's love for all things electronic. From the cover art depicting her surrounded in her room by a mess of cables and hardware to her flagrant professions of wanting to fuck her computer, this is an incredibly aptly-themed project. Not to mention that the sound echoes a lot of what is being portrayed lyrically via these booming EDM drops and rave-like ambiances shredding open some of the most upbeat songs of the year. I know I mentioned this before, but the inner EDM child locked within my spirit kept me coming back time and time again to enjoy the melodies and beat progressions!

Stepping away from the instrumentals for a second, I also want to appreciate just how personable Nina gets from a writing standpoint. She really takes you through her childhood using snapshots of past eras, and as I kid who once owned an iPod touch with a yellow Pikachu case, I can confidently say this is the lyric I related to the most in 2025. And again, being a woman making EDM seems to be a crucial component of her persona as she takes the listener through her innermost thoughts regarding growing up as a girl in a male-dominated musical universe.

Honestly, I'm blown away by how hard hitting the beats are on this thing, and the touching singing performances and lyrical gems help bolster this toward a top 10 placement. I wouldn't have it any other way.

8. Danny Brown - Stardust

I predicted this album's existence 2 years ago, and I was so right about it being as good as it turned out!

To understand why this album means so much to me, I think it's worth rewinding back to late 2023 / early 2024. See, the post-Quaranta era of Danny's career marks a notable shift in sonic direction. Despite loving that album at the time it released, I will admit that I've only sparsely gone back to it since then, and I assume that has to do with just how familiar the sound of it rings nowadays.

In 2024, Danny went on a run of fantastic features with notable collaborators such as Frost Children, Alice Longyu Gao, Femtanyl, and many more, all of which were feeding into my desire to see him drop a project fully diving into these electronic and hyperpop aesthetics that I've really been loving in modern pop music. My anticipation reached a peak when I heard him praising the latest underscores album Wallsocket (which I still love and play more often than not), and I knew it was all a matter of time until my dreams materialized.

Sure enough, I had to wait for late 2025, but I would've waited even longer for this album if needed. After hearing this, I have to say: I'm pretty sure Danny Brown is more tapped in than I am when it comes to contemporary talent. Not only does Stardust feature some of my all-time favorite artists like Quadeca, underscores, and Jane Remover, but he is also putting me onto some new faces in the process: Issbrokie has a killer verse on her track, Holly produced some of the wildest sounding beats I've heard in 2025, and taUkrainka adds some Ukrainian / Polish verses that absolutely steal the show by the album's end.

To keep it short before I ramble on any longer, I think Danny Brown found his calling with this rap-pop-electronic hybrid. Not only does he keep his lyrics precise and profound, but he's also doing so in a way that doesn't compromise on sonic creativity, and not many rappers are operating with as much flair and free-wheeling spirit as he's been showcasing lately.

7. Clipse - Let God Sort Em Out

Without a doubt one of the best rap albums of 2025.

On LGSEO, Clipse make a ferocious comeback, reclaiming their throne at the top in a way that's quite uncommon for new-age artists to do. With a stellar rollout (both before and after dropping the album), the duo managed to breathe life back into hip-hop while reminding audiences around the world what made their initial run so incredible.

First, the bars are top notch. On any of the album's songs, I feel like you can pull out 3-5 quotables, which is almost unheard of in today's age. Both Pusha T and Malice find ways to say a lot through seemingly inoffensive lines, and I truly love the way they stick to rhyming patterns for each individual song. It really feels like a resurgence of vintage rap in that regard.

Second, the production is top notch. Manned mainly by Pharrel Williams, every song sounds absolutely pristine: he manages to capture sinister ambiances, to experiment with novel ideas, and to throw it back to some of the earliest works by the Neptunes. Across the entire album, there's not even a single beat I can call out as being weak: every moment counts, every drum hit feels needed, and I have absolutely no complaints.

Third, the features are top notch. Whether it's Kendrick Lamar on "Chains & Whips", Tyler the Creator on "P.O.V" (still love the way he says peel off like an orange), or even that tremendous chorus by Stove God Cooks on "F.I.C.O", Clipse had a vision when it came to selecting a roster for the album, and every person they invited delivered in a big way. Even more minor inclusions like the spoken word passages by Stevie Wonder and The Dream felt necessary to the full listen, and I again have no notes.

If anything, this album is a testament to the talent and motivation required to make a rap album worthy of the album of the year title. And Clipse proved to have both in spades this year.

6. De La Soul - Cabin in the Sky

Truly one for the soul.

Indeed, my favorite rap album of the year comes from a defining hip-hop trio from the 80's and 90's, once again solidifying that 2025 was a year marked by impressive comeback albums from unlikely repeat faces.

Interestingly, I assumed we'd never get another De La Soul album ever again, especially not after Dave, one of the band's founding members, tragically passed away in 2023. But this would've been a shame, and the remaining duo wanted to ensure that Dave's legacy lived on in the current era. And what better way to introduce a new set of faces to some of hip-hop's most classic works than to release a masterclass unlike any other.

Cabin in the Sky is an album, but it's more importantly a metaphor best exemplified by the cover art. With Pos and Maseo carrying on the trio's legacy, they spend this album conjuring a metaphorical "cabin in the sky" where Dave is now peacefully residing in the afterlife. Narrated by Giancarlo Esposito, the album kicks off with an introduction of the many legacy artists that contributed to this album's success, including some all-time rapping legends like Killer Mike, Black Thought, Nas, Slick Rick, Common, Q-Tip... The list goes on, and the role-call segment is a brilliant way to introduce this concept.

Of course, any good album is only as great as the music behind the message, but De La Soul ensures that even those bases are covered by the time the first official song begins. Immediately, you get hit with their signature boom-bap style which punches you right in the nostalgia muscle, and some legendary producers like DJ Premier, Pete Rock, and even Dave himself contribute to making this album sound old-school but still fresh by today's standards. There are so many standout beats, it's impossible to list them all, but some of my favorites include the dance sample on "Cruel Summers Bring Fire Life", the shimmering production that makes "Yours" sound like a ray of sunshine, and the jazzy spin on "En Eff" which is pure bliss for vintage hip-hop heads.

As a post-humous album, I think the premise of a "cabin in the sky" wonderfully embodies this idea of the afterlife, and De La Soul builds up this concept so well; it really does sound like Pos and Maseo are reaching out to Dave from beyond on many of the deep cuts. And I guarantee, hearing that last song will have you in tears once you put all the pieces together.

RIP Dave, you'll live on through this album, forever.

5. Saya Gray - SAYA

Art pop is such a good genre, isn't it?

I'm a known sucker for art pop albums. I've always loved how artists like Kate Bush or Julia Holter are able to mold sound into such interesting ways, turning could-be-radio-hits into far off abstractions that are just strange enough to dodge the mainstream.

Personally, I think Canadian-Japanese artist Saya Gray handily claims art pop victory with her self-titled debut album in 2025. Even all the way back in February when this released, I remember being immediately fully immersed into the listen the minute I heard the transition between tracks 3 and 4. From that moment onward, it's just been pure admiration on my behalf as I worked to unpack the many faces concealed behind such a mysterious figure.

What's interesting is that Saya previously released some QWERTY EPs before making this album and, although they were a lot messier and difficult to envision as whole, they laid the groundwork for the unique instrumentation that can be heard all over SAYA. She truly carried over the smoothest ideas and maintained a lot of the same lyrical motifs that give her catalogue a common thread between projects.

On the instrumental side, there's a lot of variety for such a compact listen. I love the twangy guitars that make the 2 opening tracks so groovy, I love the masterful drumbeats near the albums midpoint that help to link the tracks in a way that feels so organic, I love the bits of harp scattered everywhere that offer a magical quality to the sound, I love the electric guitar explosions at the end of "Exhaust the Topic" that give me a rush of energy every time; I pretty much adore every single musical idea she throws your way across the listen.

If I had to pick a favorite component to the album though, I'd definitely say its lyricism. Despite mainly dancing around the topic of love as many artists have done for centuries now, Saya uses such creative imagery to rehash ideas that have been around forever. Whether envisioning a graveyard in her dreams where her love went to die or picturing herself melting as a puddle at her lover's feet, there are so many evocative visuals laced in the tracklist. She also tells a very compelling story from start to finish, ingeniously circling back to this line about "not springing for love" before the album reaches its conclusion.

Hey, give it a try! It won't let you down, I promise.

4. Hannah Frances - Nested in Tangles

As far as albums that have had a huge impact on my life in 2025, Nested in Tangles ranks amongst the top contenders (if not the top).

My appreciation for Hannah's music stems from her 2024 release Keeper of the Shepherd, an album that redefined my love for folk music and profound lyrical displays. Looking back at my top albums list from last year, I definitely feel like I might've lowballed this ranking a bit given just how frequently I've revisited her songs this year, especially in the lead-up to this new album.

Essentially, just a year later, we're graced with more of the same, though this time there is a noticeably warmer and more optimistic angle surrounding the album's theming. Despite a lot of the subject matter still ringing as emotional as can be, songs like "Falling from and Further" sound way more positive than the hollowed-out desperation from many of Keeper's most cathartic moments.

It also helps that the instrumentals carry many of the qualities I admired from her 2024 work, adding a warmer charm with guitarwork from Daniel Rossen on 2 major songs this time around. Even the flow from track to track feels more dynamic thanks to the added interludes that help to seal the experience nicely. You can definitely expect the usual sunny guitar tones, progressive drum patterns, saxophone runs that'll leave you wanting more, and even some electric guitars mixed with panning vocal effects that really show how creatively she was operating this year.

Hannah's lyricism is also commendable, and she carries the bulk of this project through sheer vocal talent and contemplative writing. A ballad like "Steady In The Hand" rings as one of the most tender songs I've heard in 2025 (and of all time), the heightened energy on "Surviving You" feels as dramatic as the lyrics imply, and I love the grandiose instrumental build on "Heavy Light" that resolves the album at the brightest sonic peak you can imagine.

I suppose seeing her perform many of these songs live really brought the album to life for me, and I can't pretend like I'm not hopelessly enamoured by her artistic prowess after hearing just two albums!

3. Deafheaven - Lonely People with Power

Yes. Yes! YES!!!

Okay, I might get some flack for this, but I'm going to say it anyway: I never really saw Sunbather as a decade-defining metal album during the 2010's. Don't hate me, but it's true. I could definitely commend the experimentation that Deafheaven underwent when recording an album merging black metal and shoegaze, but it's not an album I used to replay all that much. I've always admired it, but part of me always wondered whether the band could put together an even more interesting take on blackgaze...

Lonely People with Power answers the question about just how good things could get: really fucking good! I might get flack for this next statement too, but I'll be brave: this is hands-down Deafheaven's best album, and it's not even close.

It's been a while since I heard a metal album where the lyrics act as the primary appeal to me. However, the band undeniably wrote some hits, and I deeply encourage you to devote all your attention to this record when giving it a spin to actually dive into what's being said. The cover art alone is already striking enough of a visual, but they get even more precise with every lyric discussing ideas of psychosis, declining mental health, crippling loneliness, desires of warmth or intimacy, unbreakable gender roles and expectations, insurmountable addiction, repeated cycles of depravity, and the album ends things ambiguously with the protagonist embarking on this road toward recovery, though the listener is left wondering whether change is truly on the horizon.

So, yeah, the album is incredibly gripping thematically, but the tunes at the core of the music are just as entertaining as the message. The song "Doberman" kicks things off with a bang, showcasing just how strong the band is when they stick to pure black metal: the track's chorus is phenomenal, I love the phasic shifts in energy, and it's just an exceptional banger. These fiery guitar tones carry over on many tracks like the climactic ending to "Magnolia" and even the main riff driving "Amethyst" toward such a glorious midpoint.

It's also interesting to hear the band experiment with various styles of music beyond simply sticking to their comfort zone of piercing black metal. "Heathen" features a soft melodic passage that opens up the song, and I'll be the first to admit that the combination between simple acoustic and crushing metal right after just works. This style blend is also reiterated during the multiple spoken word segments that help keep the tension between the album's many moments, and I love how unpredictably some songs will just fade to utter silence without a warning. Finally, it was nice to hear them devote the last two tracks to a more typical blackgaze experience which really helps to lull you into a state of semi-consciousness as you come to terms with the beauty of what you just heard. Really, it's such a powerful finale, and a subtly emotional one too.

All in all, I can't really throw this album any further praise beyond the obvious: metal album of the year!

2. The Weeknd - Hurry Up Tomorrow

Pure cinema.

I'll be the first to admit that I was slightly worried going into this album when I heard the singles leading up to it, but all of those worries melted away instantly when "Wake Me Up" transitioned into "Cry for Me" so jarringly on that first listen.

What Abel Tesfaye achieved on Hurry Up Tomorrow is exceptional. This is the 3rd and last installment in this latest trilogy of albums he put out in the 2020's, and I can't say that he faltered even once during the whole process. From redefining synthpop in a new era with After Hours to conceptualizing an entire purgatory radio-state on Dawn FM, there's nothing Abel can't do.

On this final leg of the trilogy, the emphasis seems to be conducting a cinematic finish to the saga, and Abel succeeds with flying colors thanks to the legendary cast of producers he assembled to assist in making Hurry Up Tomorrow sound so polished: Justice kills it on "Wake Me Up" as they flip that classic guitar groove on "Thriller" into something new, Max Martin on "Open Hearts" leads the track to a place of pop perfection, Metro Boomin and Travis Scott help bridge the gap between classic and modern soundscapes, Giorgio Moroder even makes an appearance on "Big Sleep" to drive home the dramatic turning point with a heavy dose of shimmering synths, but you get the idea by now.

Not only did Abel recruit some of the best producers in the game, but he also curated a feature list precisely to bring to light his vision of the end. Everyone contributes to this album in a positive way: Anitta carries the funky ambiance on "São Paulo" to a new dimension, Travis Scott on "Reflections Laughing" plays the role of Abel's darkest side with a low pitch delivery whereas Florence + The Machine adds some divine backing vocals in the leadup to the darkness, Future on "Enjoy The Show" absolutely smashes his emotional verse, Lana Del Rey stuns on "The Abyss" with her vocal range, and even Playboi Carti on "Timeless" gives his best performance in years!

However, on an album 80-minutes in length, it's safe to say that Abel himself takes up the bulk of the runtime, and he does not disappoint even slightly. Throughout the listen, he offers some of his personal writing ever, he gives you one stunning vocal performance after the next, he carries you seamlessly from track to track thanks to genius musical ideas and a plethora of short segues, and it's just an impressive foray into the most creative corners of his mind.

I'm a bit perplexed that this album was a bit sidelined by the public, but don't be fooled by the shy reception: giving this album your time will only feel rewarding in the end. As a long-time fan of The Weeknd as a musical persona, I couldn't imagine a better finale to the infamous character's legacy.

1. Samia - Bloodless

I debated for a long time before opting to put this at the number one spot, but it feels right the more I look back at this ranking.

In a year where I spent a great deal of time confronting novelty, having something constant to act as a baseline to my life felt comforting. Somehow, the stars aligned in a way that Samia became that reference point for me in 2025.

I discovered Samia in 2023 when she put out her album Honey, and I remembered really clicking with that record's sadness at the time. Shortly after, I dug my way back to 2020's The Baby, and I liked the poppier elements of that album even more than the dreariness of its successor. Naturally, having been primed on her career before the leadup to Bloodless back in January, I was thrilled to be entering a new era blindly.

However, I knew we'd be hearing something special the moment "Bovine Excision" released as the lead single. On top of highlighting the natural country appeal to Samia's voice in a way that hadn't been taken advantage of previously, it features this really cryptic and abstract lyricism which forms the bulk of what makes Bloodless so appealing to me. The track sets this central theme about being drained, sucked dry, and completely empty inside, and Samia runs with this idea throughout the remainder of the tracklist with ease.

As singles kept steadily releasing during the beginning of the year, I found myself increasingly anticipating the full album. "Lizard" was another track which marked me thanks to its sparkly guitar tone, "Hole in a Frame" hit me hard due to its nature being infused with several potent doses of melancholy, and dropping the 6-minute closing track "Pants" as a single might be the boldest musical move of the year. It's definitely not an accessible track with its lack of structure, odd breakpoint build, and phasic nature, but it had me so intrigued to hear more; I was on the edge of my seat waiting for the release date to come strolling by.

When that day finally came, I sat down and listened, only for all my anticipation to feel validated on the spot. Perhaps Bloodless isn't the most ambitious nor technically proficient album I've ever heard, but it's rare to come across gems like these where every song feels special. I can even go as far as saying that the great deal of songs on this album very well had the potential to be singles. Take "Dare" with its low-pitched groove and slow jam nature that displays Samia's vocal talent tremendously as she carries the weight of emotion so well through every lyric. You can also take "North Poles" with its fascinating build as it effortlessly weaves between choruses, sort of acting like a beacon of light amidst a listen that hovers around gloom most of the time. I even love the small interlude-like moments such as "Craziest Person" which help unify the album as a whole.

And yes, something that would be criminal for me to neglect is just how seamlessly each track glides into the next. Sometimes the transitions come across as abrupt and jarring, but the underlying rhythm that the tracks carry just makes perfect sense in the context of a full listen. Suddenly, hearing "Pants" again after acquiring this newfound context elevates it to a new height, and I was right to be intrigued about it initially.

I guess I can end things off by complimenting Samia's lyricism, vocal talent, but also the way her and the band she works with sound completely in sync the entire time. Instrumentally, everyone is playing as a unit, and that offers a lot of charm to the listen. I'm a big fan of music that sounds like it was made in a fun condition because it more often than not translates into the quality of the record.

All I'm saying is that I'm truly in awe at how wonderfully Bloodless turned out, and it's my most played album of the year for a reason.
